What is a Force?

A force is an interaction between two objects that can change the motion of an object. In mechanics we treat forces as vectors: each has magnitude and direction. Forces result from interactions (contact or field) and always act on a specific object.

The Nine Types of Forces — Catalog

Below are the common forces encountered in introductory mechanics. Each entry includes the origin, simple formula (where applicable), typical AP Physics 1 contexts, and a short practice problem with solution.

1. Gravitational Force (Weight)

Near Earth's surface weight is modeled as W = mg directed downward. This is an approximation of the universal law when the separation is near Earth's radius. Weight is the force of Earth on an object and is distinct from mass.

AP contexts: apparent weight, inclines, normal comparisons.

AP Problem — Apparent Weight
Problem: A 70.0 kg person stands on a scale in an elevator accelerating upward at 1.6 m/s². What does the scale read?
Solution: N - mg = ma → N = m(g + a) = 70(9.80 + 1.6) = 798 N.

2. Normal Force

The normal force is the contact force a surface exerts on an object, always perpendicular to the surface. On an incline with no vertical acceleration, N = mg cos θ. Normal force changes when other vertical components are present.

AP Problem — Normal on Incline
Problem: A 10.0-kg block sits on a 30° incline. Find N.
Solution: N = mg cos30° = 10×9.8×cos30° ≈ 84.9 N.

3. Friction (static & kinetic)

Friction models tangential contact interactions. Static friction resists motion up to f_s ≤ μ_s N. Kinetic friction during sliding is f_k = μ_k N. Always check whether static friction can hold before switching to kinetic.

AP Problem — Sliding Crate
Problem: A 10.0 kg crate is pushed with 60.0 N. μ_s = 0.50, μ_k = 0.35. Does it move? If so, a?
Solution: N = mg = 98 N. f_s,max = 0.5×98 = 49 N. 60 > 49 → it moves. f_k = 0.35×98 = 34.3 N. Net F = 60 − 34.3 = 25.7 N → a = 25.7/10 = 2.57 m/s².

4. Tension

Tension is the pulling force in a rope or string. For ideal massless ropes and frictionless pulleys, the magnitude of tension is the same everywhere in the rope. Model each mass with its own FBD and connect via the rope constraint.

AP Problem — Pulley and Masses
Problem: m1 = 4.0 kg on table (μ_k = 0.16), m2 = 3.0 kg hanging. Find a and T (sketch provided).
Sketch solution: m2 g − T = m2 a; T − f_k = m1 a. Solve the two equations for a and T.

5. Spring Force (Hooke's law)

An ideal spring exerts a restoring force F_s = −k x. The minus sign indicates the force is opposite displacement. Combine Hooke's law with energy (½ k x²) when useful for AP-style reasoning.

AP Problem — Spring + friction energy
Problem: A 2.0-kg block attached to k = 200 N/m spring is compressed x = 0.20 m on a surface with μ_k = 0.10. How far from equilibrium before stopping?
Outline: ½ k x² = μ_k m g d → solve for d.

6. Applied Force

Any push or pull by an agent (person, machine) is an applied force. Always state its direction and point of contact. If applied at an angle, resolve into components and account for vertical component altering N.

AP Problem — Pulling at angle
Problem: A person pulls a 12.0-kg suitcase with 30.0 N at 20° above horizontal across frictionless floor. Find acceleration.
Solution: F_x = 30 cos20°, a = F_x / m.

7. Drag (Air resistance — quadratic model)

At moderate-to-high Reynolds numbers drag scales quadratically with speed: F_d = ½ C_d A v², acting opposite velocity. Terminal speed occurs when ΣF = 0 so mg = ½ C_d A v_t².

AP Problem — Terminal speed
Problem: Sphere m = 0.20 kg, C_d = 0.47, A = 0.01 m². Find v_t.
Solution: v_t = √(2 m g / (C_d A)).

8. Electric Force

Coulomb's law: F = k q₁ q₂ / r², acting along the line joining charges. While mechanics-focused, this is included as a field force example.

AP Problem — Coulomb magnitude
Problem: +2.0 μC and −3.0 μC separated by 0.40 m. Find force on +2.0 μC.
Solution: F = k (2.0e-6)(3.0e-6)/0.40² ≈ 0.34 N toward negative charge.

9. Magnetic Force

A moving charge in a magnetic field experiences F = q v B sinθ, perpendicular to velocity and field. This is a field force example; typically used qualitatively in AP1 problems.

AP Problem — Moving charge
Problem: A proton (q = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C) moves at 2.0×10⁶ m/s perpendicular to a 0.20 T field. Find F.
Solution: F = q v B = 1.6e-19 × 2.0e6 × 0.20 = 6.4×10⁻¹⁴ N.

Newton’s Three Laws of Motion

First Law — Inertia

An object remains at rest or moves at constant velocity unless acted upon by a net external force. In AP problems, "no acceleration" implies ΣF = 0 — check components carefully.

AP Problem — Hockey Puck

A puck slides on ice and enters a patch with μ_k = 0.020. If v₀ = 6.0 m/s, how far before stopping? Use a = −μ_k g and v² = v₀² + 2 a x.

Second Law — ΣF = ma

Acceleration is proportional to net external force and inversely proportional to mass. Always apply ΣF = ma in components, and be explicit about sign conventions.

AP Problem — Two Blocks and Rope

Two blocks 5.0 kg and 3.0 kg connected on frictionless surface pulled by 20 N. a = 20 / (5+3) = 2.5 m/s². T = m₂ a = 3 × 2.5 = 7.5 N.

Third Law — Action–Reaction

For every force (action) there is an equal and opposite force (reaction) acting on a different object. They never cancel on the same object; treat each body separately.

AP Problem — Recoil

A 4.0 kg rifle fires a 0.020 kg bullet at 300 m/s. Using momentum conservation find rifle recoil speed: v_r = −(m_b v_b)/m_r.

Free-Body Diagrams — Particle FBDs (AP-Physics-1)

AP Physics 1 favors the particle model for many mechanics problems: represent the object as a point mass (particle) and draw forces acting on that point. This simplifies rotational and size effects and aligns with typical exam items.

Particle FBD: Rules & Construction (AP-1 aligned)

  1. Choose the system (single object) and treat it as a particle (dot). Ignore dimensions — particle FBD focuses on external forces only.
  2. Draw coordinate axes. Use axes that simplify the problem (often along incline and perpendicular or horizontal/vertical).
  3. Show all external forces acting on the particle. Common labels: W (weight, mg, downward), N (normal, perpendicular to surface), f (friction, along surface, oppose motion), T (tension, along rope), F_app (applied), F_d (drag), F_e (electric), F_m (magnetic), F_s (spring).
  4. Represent each force by an arrow starting at the particle and pointing in the correct direction. Do not draw internal forces (those between parts of the system) or hypothetical reaction pairs that act on other bodies.
  5. Label magnitudes or expressions where useful (e.g., W = mg, f_k = μ_k N, F_d = ½ C_d A v²). Keep symbolic labels when solving analytically.
  6. Apply ΣF = ma in components. Break vectors into chosen axes, sum components, substitute knowns, and solve for unknowns. For equilibrium, set ΣF_x = 0 and ΣF_y = 0.
  7. Check units and limiting cases. Does your answer reduce to sensible limits (e.g., μ→0, a→0) and do units match?
AP tip: For AP free-response, use the particle model unless the prompt explicitly requires extended-body analysis (torques, rotation). Label forces clearly and state your axis choices — exam graders look for clear diagrams and ΣF setups.

Practice Problems (start)

A set of AP-style problems. Solutions are given; longer multi-step solutions and two video placeholders live in Part 2.

Problem 1

A 5.0-kg block is pulled across a rough horizontal surface with a force of 25 N at 20° above horizontal. If μ_k = 0.25, find the acceleration. (Particle FBD)

Solution: F_x = 25 cos20° = 23.5 N; F_y = 25 sin20° = 8.55 N. N = mg − F_y = 49 − 8.55 = 40.45 N. f_k = μ_k N = 0.25×40.45 = 10.11 N. Net horizontal = 23.5 − 10.11 = 13.39 N. a = 13.39/5 = 2.68 m/s².

Problem 2

Two blocks, 8.0 kg and 2.0 kg, are connected by a rope over a pulley. The 8.0-kg block rests on a horizontal surface with μ_k = 0.10. Find the acceleration and the tension. (Particle FBDs for each mass.)

Solution: f_k = 0.10×(8×9.8) = 7.84 N. Net force = m_hanging g − f_k = 2×9.8 − 7.84 = 11.76 N. Total mass = 10 kg → a = 11.76/10 = 1.176 m/s². T = m_hanging g − m_hanging a = 19.6 − 2.352 = 17.248 N.

Problem 3

A 0.40-kg object falls vertically. Drag force is quadratic: F_d = 0.20 v². Find terminal speed.

Solution: At terminal velocity, mg = k v². v = √(mg/k) = √(0.40×9.8 / 0.20) = √19.6 = 4.43 m/s.

Problem 4

A spring gun launches a 0.10-kg projectile using k = 800 N/m spring compressed 0.050 m. Neglect friction. Find launch speed.

Solution: ½ k x² = ½ m v² → v = √(k/m) x = √(800/0.10) × 0.050 = √8000 × 0.05 = √400 = 20 m/s.
