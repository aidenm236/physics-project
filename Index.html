What is a Force?

A force is an interaction between two objects. It can change the object’s velocity by producing an acceleration. Every force has an agent (the object that causes it) and a receiver (the object it acts on). Forces are vectors, so direction is as important as magnitude.

The Nine Types of Forces

These are the forces discussed in this chapter, grouped as contact forces and field forces. Each description is paired with a practice problem at the AP Physics 1 level.

1. Gravitational Force (Weight)

Weight is the force of Earth’s gravity on an object: W = mg. It is always directed vertically downward. Distinguish carefully between mass (a scalar property) and weight (a vector force). Apparent weight depends on acceleration: in an elevator accelerating upward, the normal force (scale reading) exceeds mg.

AP Problem — Apparent Weight

A 70.0-kg student stands on a scale in an elevator accelerating upward at 2.0 m/s². What does the scale read? Solution: ΣF = ma → N - mg = ma → N = m(g + a) = 70(9.8 + 2.0) = 826 N.

2. Normal Force

A surface pushes on an object perpendicular to itself. For an object on an incline of angle θ with no vertical acceleration, N = mg cosθ. If an extra vertical push or pull is applied, N adjusts accordingly.

AP Problem — Normal on Incline

A 10.0-kg block rests on a 30° incline. What is the normal force? Solution: N = mg cosθ = 10(9.8)(cos30°) = 84.9 N.

3. Friction

Friction opposes relative motion. Static friction resists starting motion up to a maximum of μ_s N. Kinetic friction resists sliding with f_k = μ_k N. Static friction adjusts to match applied force until its maximum is exceeded.

AP Problem — Sliding Crate

A 12.0-kg box on a horizontal floor is pushed with 50.0 N. If μ_s = 0.40 and μ_k = 0.30, will it move? If so, find a. Solution: N = 12(9.8) = 117.6 N. f_s,max = 0.40×117.6 = 47.0 N. Since 50.0 > 47.0, it moves. f_k = 0.30×117.6 = 35.3 N. Net F = 50.0 - 35.3 = 14.7 N → a = 14.7/12 = 1.23 m/s².

4. Tension

Tension is the pulling force transmitted by a rope or string, always directed away from the object. In ideal ropes (massless, inextensible, frictionless pulleys), the tension is uniform.

AP Problem — Two Masses and Pulley

A 4.0-kg mass on a frictionless table is attached by a string over a pulley to a 3.0-kg hanging mass. Find the acceleration and the tension. Solution: m2 g - T = m2 a, T = m1 a. Solve → a = (m2 g)/(m1 + m2) = 3×9.8/7 = 4.2 m/s². Then T = m1 a = 16.8 N.

5. Spring Force

A spring stretched or compressed by x exerts F = -kx. The minus sign means the force is restoring. Hooke’s law holds only within the spring’s elastic limit.

AP Problem — Mass and Spring

A 0.50-kg block is attached to a horizontal spring with k = 200 N/m. The spring is stretched 0.10 m and released. Ignoring friction, what is the acceleration at that moment? Solution: F = kx = 200×0.10 = 20 N → a = F/m = 20/0.50 = 40 m/s².

6. Applied Force

Any push or pull by an agent is called an applied force. Its direction and effect depend on the situation. For example, pulling at an upward angle both helps move forward and reduces the normal force.

AP Problem — Pulling at Angle

A 20.0-kg box is pulled with 100 N at 30° above horizontal across a rough surface (μ_k = 0.25). Find the acceleration. Solution: Components: F_x = 86.6 N, F_y = 50.0 N. N = mg - F_y = 196 - 50 = 146 N. f_k = μ_k N = 36.5 N. Net horizontal = 86.6 - 36.5 = 50.1 N. a = 50.1/20 = 2.51 m/s².

7. Drag (Air Resistance)

Drag resists motion through air, opposing velocity. At higher speeds, it is modeled as quadratic: F_d = ½ C_d A v². Terminal speed occurs when mg = F_d.

AP Problem — Terminal Velocity

A 0.20-kg sphere with C_d = 0.50 and A = 0.010 m² falls through air. Find terminal velocity. Solution: v_t = √(2mg/(C_d A)) = √(2×0.20×9.8/(0.5×0.010)) ≈ 28 m/s.

8. Electric Force

The electric force is a field force between charged objects, given by Coulomb’s law: F = k q₁q₂ / r². It acts along the line connecting the charges, attractive if opposite in sign, repulsive if alike.

AP Problem — Coulomb’s Law

Two charges +2.0 μC and −3.0 μC are 0.40 m apart. Find the force on the +2.0 μC charge. Solution: F = (8.99×10⁹)(2.0e-6)(3.0e-6)/(0.40²) = 0.34 N, directed toward the negative charge.

9. Magnetic Force

A moving charge in a magnetic field experiences F = qvB sinθ, perpendicular to both velocity and field (right-hand rule). While detailed magnetism is later in physics, here it is presented as an example of a field force.

AP Problem — Charge in Magnetic Field

A proton (q = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C) moves at 2.0×10⁶ m/s perpendicular to a 0.20 T magnetic field. Find the magnetic force. Solution: F = qvB = (1.6e-19)(2.0e6)(0.20) = 6.4×10⁻¹⁴ N.

Newton’s Three Laws of Motion

First Law — Inertia

An object remains at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted on by a net external force. Inertia is the property of matter to resist changes in motion. Remember that "no acceleration" does not mean "no forces," but rather that forces balance to ΣF = 0.

AP Problem — Hockey Puck

A hockey puck slides on ice with negligible friction. It hits a patch where kinetic friction is μₖ = 0.020. If the puck’s speed is initially 6.0 m/s, how far does it travel before stopping? Solution: a = −μₖg = −0.20 m/s². Using v² = v₀² + 2ax → 0 = 36 + 2(−0.20)x → x = 90 m.

Second Law — ΣF = ma

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net external force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. This is the quantitative heart of Newtonian mechanics. Always apply ΣF = ma in component form.

AP Problem — Two Blocks and Rope

Two blocks, 5.0 kg and 3.0 kg, are connected by a rope on a frictionless table. A 20 N force pulls on the 5.0-kg block. Find the acceleration and the tension. Solution: ΣF = 20 = (5+3)a → a = 2.5 m/s². T = m₂a = 3×2.5 = 7.5 N.

Third Law — Action-Reaction

For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force. These forces act on different bodies. Action–reaction pairs never cancel because they act on different objects.

AP Problem — Pushing on a Wall

A person pushes on a wall with 150 N. The wall pushes back with 150 N. If forces are equal and opposite, why doesn’t the wall move? Solution: The wall experiences 150 N but is attached to Earth with enormous mass, so its acceleration is negligible. The equal force on the person is why their muscles feel the resistance.

Free-Body Diagrams (FBDs)

A Free-Body Diagram is a drawing that shows all the forces acting on a single object, represented as arrows. It is the key tool for applying Newton’s Laws.

Steps to Draw an FBD

  1. Isolate the object of interest — represent it as a simple box or dot.
  2. Draw a coordinate system if helpful (x and y axes).
  3. Represent each force acting on the object as an arrow starting at the object.
  4. Label each arrow clearly (W, N, f, T, Fapp, etc.).
  5. Make the arrow length reflect relative magnitudes if possible.

Practice Problems

Use these to test mastery.

Problem 1

A 5.0-kg block is pulled across a rough horizontal surface with a force of 25 N at 20° above horizontal. If μ_k = 0.25, find the acceleration. Draw an FBD.

Solution: Horizontal force = 25cos20° = 23.5 N, vertical component reduces normal. N = 49 − 8.55 = 40.5 N. Friction = 0.25×40.5 = 10.1 N. Net horizontal = 23.5 − 10.1 = 13.4 N. a = 13.4/5 = 2.68 m/s².

Problem 2

Two blocks, 8.0 kg and 2.0 kg, are connected by a rope over a pulley. The 8.0-kg block rests on a horizontal surface with μ_k = 0.10. Find the acceleration and the tension. Include an FBD for each block.

Solution: f_k = 0.10×(8×9.8) = 7.84 N. Net F = (2×9.8) − 7.84 = 11.8 N. Total mass = 10 kg → a = 11.8/10 = 1.18 m/s². T = m₂g − m₂a = 19.6 − 2.36 = 17.2 N.

Problem 3 (with video)

A 1.50-kg cart is pushed with a constant horizontal force of 6.0 N across a frictionless surface. What is its acceleration? If it starts from rest, what is its speed after 4.0 s?

Problem 4 (with video)

A 0.40-kg ball is dropped from rest. Air resistance is negligible. Find its velocity after 2.5 s. Then, if drag (quadratic) acts and terminal velocity is 20 m/s, explain qualitatively how velocity differs.
