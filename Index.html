What is a Force?

A force is an interaction between two objects that can change the motion of an object. In mechanics we treat forces as vectors: each has magnitude and direction. Forces result from interactions (contact or field) and always act on a specific object.

The Nine Types of Forces — Catalog

Below are the common forces encountered in introductory mechanics. Each entry includes the origin, simple formula (where applicable), typical AP Physics 1 contexts, and a short practice problem with solution.

1. Gravitational Force (Weight)

Near Earth's surface weight is modeled as W = mg directed downward. This is an approximation of the universal law when the separation is near Earth's radius.

2. Normal Force

The normal force is the contact force a surface exerts on an object, always perpendicular to the surface.

3. Friction

Friction resists motion along surfaces.

4. Tension

Tension is the pulling force in a rope or string.

5. Spring Force (Hooke's law)

An ideal spring exerts a restoring force F_s = −k x.

6. Applied Force

Any push or pull by an agent is an applied force.

7. Drag

Drag opposes motion through a fluid.

8. Electric Force

Coulomb's law governs electric forces between charges.

9. Magnetic Force

A moving charge in a magnetic field experiences a force perpendicular to velocity and field.

Newton’s Three Laws of Motion

First Law — Inertia

An object remains at rest or moves at constant velocity unless acted upon by a net external force.

Second Law — ΣF = ma

Acceleration is proportional to net external force and inversely proportional to mass.

Third Law — Action–Reaction

For every force (action) there is an equal and opposite force acting on a different object.

Free-Body Diagrams — Particle FBDs (AP-Physics-1)

AP Physics 1 favors the particle model for many mechanics problems: represent the object as a point mass (particle) and draw forces acting on that point. This simplifies rotational and size effects and aligns with typical exam items.

Particle FBD: Rules & Construction (AP-1 aligned)

  1. Choose the system (single object) and treat it as a particle (dot). Ignore dimensions — particle FBD focuses on external forces only.
  2. Draw coordinate axes. Use axes that simplify the problem (often along incline and perpendicular or horizontal/vertical).
  3. Show all external forces acting on the particle. Common labels: W (weight, mg, downward), N (normal, perpendicular to surface), f (friction, along surface, oppose motion), T (tension, along rope), F_app (applied), F_d (drag), F_e (electric), F_m (magnetic), F_s (spring).
  4. Represent each force by an arrow starting at the particle and pointing in the correct direction. Do not draw internal forces (those between parts of the system) or hypothetical reaction pairs that act on other bodies.
  5. Label magnitudes or expressions where useful (e.g., W = mg, f_k = μ_k N, F_d = ½ C_d A v²). Keep symbolic labels when solving analytically.
  6. Apply ΣF = ma in components. Break vectors into chosen axes, sum components, substitute knowns, and solve for unknowns. For equilibrium, set ΣF_x = 0 and ΣF_y = 0.
  7. Check units and limiting cases. Does your answer reduce to sensible limits (e.g., μ→0, a→0) and do units match?
AP tip: For AP free-response, use the particle model unless the prompt explicitly requires extended-body analysis (torques, rotation). Label forces clearly and state your axis choices — exam graders look for clear diagrams and ΣF setups.

Problem 1

A 5.0-kg block is pulled across a rough horizontal surface with a force of 25 N at 20° above horizontal. If μ_k = 0.25, find the acceleration. (Particle FBD)

Solution: F_x = 25 cos20° = 23.5 N; F_y = 25 sin20° = 8.55 N. N = mg − F_y = 49 − 8.55 = 40.45 N. f_k = μ_k N = 0.25×40.45 = 10.11 N. Net horizontal = 23.5 − 10.11 = 13.39 N. a = 13.39/5 = 2.68 m/s².
