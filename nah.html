What is a Force?

A force is an interaction between two objects that can change the motion of an object. In mechanics we treat forces as vectors: each has magnitude and direction. Forces result from interactions (contact or field) and always act on a specific object.

The Nine Types of Forces — Catalog

Below are the common forces encountered in introductory mechanics. Each entry includes the origin, simple formula (where applicable), typical AP Physics 1 contexts, and a short practice problem with solution.

1. Gravitational Force (Weight)

Near Earth's surface weight is modeled as W = mg directed downward. Weight is the force of Earth on an object and distinct from mass.

AP Problem — Apparent Weight
A 70 kg person stands on a scale accelerating upward at 1.6 m/s². N = m(g + a) = 70(9.8 + 1.6) = 798 N.

2. Normal Force

The normal force is the contact force a surface exerts on an object, always perpendicular to the surface.

AP Problem — Normal on Incline
A 10 kg block sits on 30° incline. N = mg cos30° = 10×9.8×cos30° ≈ 84.9 N.

3. Friction (static & kinetic)

Static friction resists motion up to f_s ≤ μ_s N. Kinetic friction: f_k = μ_k N.

AP Problem — Sliding Crate
10 kg crate pushed with 60 N, μ_s = 0.5, μ_k = 0.35. Moves, a = 2.57 m/s².

4. Tension

Tension is pulling force in a rope/string. Equal magnitude in ideal ropes.

AP Problem — Pulley
m1 = 4 kg, m2 = 3 kg, μ_k=0.16 → solve m2 g − T = m2 a; T − f_k = m1 a.

5. Spring Force (Hooke's law)

Ideal spring exerts restoring force F = -k x.

AP Problem — Spring + friction energy
½ k x² = μ_k m g d → solve for d.

6. Applied Force

Any push or pull by an agent is an applied force.

AP Problem — Pulling at angle
12 kg suitcase, 30 N at 20° → a = (30 cos20)/12.

7. Drag (Air resistance — quadratic)

Quadratic drag: F_d = ½ C_d A v².

AP Problem — Terminal speed
m=0.2kg, C_d=0.47, A=0.01→v=√(2mg/C_dA).

8. Electric Force

Coulomb’s law: F = k q₁ q₂ / r².

AP Problem — Coulomb magnitude
+2μC and −3μC separated 0.4 m → F = 0.34 N.

9. Magnetic Force

Magnetic: F = q v B sinθ.

AP Problem — Moving charge
Proton, 2e6 m/s, B=0.2T → 6.4×10⁻¹⁴ N.

Newton’s Three Laws of Motion

First Law — Inertia

ΣF = 0 means constant velocity or rest.

Second Law — ΣF = ma

Acceleration is proportional to net external force.

Third Law — Action–Reaction

For every force there is an equal and opposite force on a different body.

Free-Body Diagrams — Particle FBDs (AP Physics 1)

Follow these rules to construct AP-aligned particle FBDs:

  1. Represent the object as a dot.
  2. Draw coordinate axes aligned with motion.
  3. Show all external forces: W, N, f, T, F_app, F_d, etc.
  4. Arrows start at the particle, pointing in correct directions.
  5. Label magnitudes symbolically.
  6. Apply ΣF = ma in components.
  7. Check units and limiting behavior.

Practice Problems

Problem 1

A 5.0-kg block is pulled across a rough surface with 25 N at 20°. μ_k = 0.25.

Solution: a = 2.68 m/s².

Problem 2

Two blocks 8 kg and 2 kg, μ_k = 0.10 → find a and T.

Solution: a = 1.18 m/s², T = 17.25 N.

Problem 3

0.40-kg object falls; F_d = 0.20 v² → find v_t.

Knowns: m = 0.40 kg, Fd = 0.20 v², g = 9.8 m/s².

Unknown: Terminal velocity vt.

Solution steps:

  1. At terminal velocity, ΣF = 0 → mg = Fd.
  2. Substitute: mg = 0.20 v².
  3. Solve v = √(mg / 0.20) = √(0.4×9.8 / 0.20) ≈ 4.43 m/s.

When describing: “The downward weight equals the upward drag at terminal velocity.”

Problem 4

A 2.0 kg object is pushed horizontally with 6 N. Friction coefficient μk = 0.25. Find acceleration and draw a particle FBD.

Knowns: m = 2.0 kg, μk = 0.25, F = 6 N.

Unknowns: a, fk.

Solution steps:

  1. Compute fk = μkN = μk mg = 0.25×2×9.8 = 4.9 N.
  2. Apply ΣFx = F − fk = ma → a = (6 − 4.9)/2 = 0.55 m/s².
  3. Particle FBD: dot with rightward 6 N, leftward 4.9 N friction, upward N = 19.6 N, downward W = 19.6 N.

When explaining: “Horizontally, net force is small because friction nearly cancels the push.”
